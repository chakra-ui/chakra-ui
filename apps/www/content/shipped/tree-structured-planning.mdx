[Tree-Structured Planning](https://treestructuredplanning.com/) is a shared
canvas where developers and their AI coding agents plan software as a visual
tree, turning the plan a chat thread can't hold into branches that each act as a
focused workspace while still seeing the whole system. It's built for engineers
who want what gets built to match what they actually scoped.

Steven Secreti is the founder and lead engineer behind it.

> "I've been using Chakra in every React project I've built for the past three
> years," he says. "I discovered it back in v2 and never looked back, so by the
> time I started this product, Chakra was less a decision and more a default I'd
> already earned through experience."

"Two things kept me loyal that whole time," he says. "The first is the component
catalog that ships with it. There's a genuinely complete set of accessible,
composable primitives in the box, so I'm never stuck hand-building a dialog or a
menu from scratch before I can get to the actual work. The second, and the
bigger one, is that Chakra gives you limitless ability to customize everything
to your liking and extend the library for your own project. It never boxes you
in."

"v3 sharpened that even further," he says. "Its semantic token layer let me
define our entire design language, a dark-first system we call _Obsidian
Pastels_, in one place, and its recipe system let me expose components as typed
contracts instead of loose JSX."

"Our codebase follows a principle that **the right way should be the only way
that compiles**, and Chakra v3's tokens and recipes were what made that
achievable on the frontend in a way no other option was."

Asked what Chakra let him do better than expected, he points to slot recipes.

"Slot recipes, easily," he says. "I went in expecting to hand-roll most of our
multi-part components, the tree node cards, the canvas toolbar, the inspector
panels. Chakra v3's slot recipes let me define all of that, every slot, every
variant, every interactive state, in a single recipe file using semantic tokens,
and typegen generated types so the slot names are validated at compile time."

"The payoff I didn't see coming was at the consumer level," he says. "Once a
recipe was proven, adding a new variant, or even a whole new component, became a
one or two file change following a visible pattern instead of something where
you had to understand five files first. That turned theming from the thing I
expected to babysit into the thing that made every new feature obviously easy."

Moving to v3 came with one snag.

"The only thing that tripped me up was my own muscle memory," he says. "After
three years on v2, my fingers knew the old APIs, so when I moved to v3 I kept
reaching for the way I used to do things, before catching myself. **The
framework wasn't the problem. My habits were.**"

"I worked through it the boring but effective way," he says. "I sat down with
the v3 docs, rebuilt a couple of components the proper v3 way, using
createSystem, semantic tokens, and recipes, until the new patterns replaced the
old reflexes. A week in, v3 felt more natural than v2 ever had, and I stopped
missing the old way entirely."

His advice for a developer considering Chakra:

"I'd tell them to go for it, and to commit to the token and recipe system from
day one," he says. "I think the biggest mistake you can make with Chakra is to
use it like a box of pre-styled components and reach for inline styles the
moment you need something custom. You end up with the same scattered,
unmaintainable styling you'd have had without it."

"The real leverage is treating the theme as a contract," he says. "Define your
tokens, build your variants as recipes, and let everything downstream consume
those. We built a fairly opinionated, dark-first design system and a dense
canvas UI on top of v3, and it never once felt like we were fighting the
framework to get there."

> "I think Chakra is the strongest option out there right now."
