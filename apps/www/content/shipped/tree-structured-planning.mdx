Steven Secreti is the founder and lead engineer of
[Tree-Structured Planning](https://treestructuredplanning.com/), a shared canvas
where developers and their AI coding agents plan software as a visual tree. Each
branch acts as a focused workspace while still seeing the whole system, so what
a chat thread can't hold becomes a plan you can actually navigate. It's built
for engineers who want what gets built to match what they actually scoped. The
whole frontend runs on Chakra UI.

## Why Chakra

For Steven, choosing Chakra wasn't really a choice. _"I've been reaching for
Chakra in every React project I've built for the past 3 years,"_ he says. _"By
the time I started this product Chakra was less a decision and more a default
I'd already earned through experience."_ Two things kept him loyal: a genuinely
complete catalog of accessible, composable primitives, and the freedom to
customize and extend without ever feeling boxed in.

Chakra v3 sharpened both. Its semantic token layer let him define the product's
entire design language, a dark-first system he calls _"Obsidian Pastels,"_ in
one place, and its recipe system let him expose components as typed contracts
instead of loose JSX. That mapped exactly onto a principle his codebase lives
by: _"the right way should be the only way that compiles."_

## The surprise

The biggest surprise was slot recipes. Steven expected to hand-roll his
multi-part components, the tree node cards, the canvas toolbar, the inspector
panels, and wire every size and variant state by hand. Instead he defined all of
it in a single recipe file with typegen validating slot names at compile time.
The payoff he didn't see coming was downstream: adding a new variant or a whole
new component became a one-to-two-file change following a visible pattern.

> That turned theming from the thing I expected to babysit into the thing that
> made every new feature obviously easy.

## The friction

The only thing that tripped him up was his own muscle memory. After three years
on v2, his fingers kept reaching for the old APIs. He fixed it the boring,
effective way, sat down with the v3 docs and rebuilt a couple of components the
proper way until the new patterns replaced the old reflexes. A week in, v3 felt
more natural than v2 ever had.

## The advice

His advice to other developers? Commit to the token and recipe system from day
one. _"Treat the theme as a contract,"_ he says. _"Do that and the library
scales with you."_
