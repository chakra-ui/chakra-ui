## 👋🏽 A bit of context

[Tree-Structured Planning](https://treestructuredplanning.com/) is a shared
canvas where developers and their AI coding agents plan software as a visual
tree, turning the plan a chat thread can't hold into branches that each act as a
focused workspace while still seeing the whole system. It's built for engineers
who want what gets built to match what they actually scoped.

Steven Secreti is the founder and lead engineer behind it.

<QuoteCard
  quote="I discovered Chakra back in v2 and never looked back."
  image="/shipped/steven-secreti.png"
/>

He'd already been building with Chakra for years, so by the time he started this
product, it wasn't a difficult decision to make. He chose Chakra because it
worked well from experience.

## Why Chakra became the default

Two things kept him loyal to that decision. First, the component catalog. Chakra
comes with accessible, composable primitives already built, so he doesn't have
to build a dialog or a menu from scratch before starting the real work. Second,
and more important, Chakra lets him customize and extend everything the way he
wants. "It never boxes you in," he says.

<QuoteCard
  quote="v3 made it easier to define our entire design language in one place and expose components as typed contracts instead of loose JSX."
  image="/shipped/steven-secreti.png"
/>

v3 made that even better. Its semantic tokens let him define their whole design
language in one place, a dark-first system they call _Obsidian Pastels_. Its
recipe system let him turn components into typed contracts instead of loose JSX.

Their codebase follows one rule: **the right way should be the only way that
compiles**. Chakra v3's tokens and recipes made that possible on the frontend.
Nothing else could.

## The power of slot recipes

We asked him what surprised him most about Chakra, and he pointed to slot
recipes.

<QuoteCard
  quote="Chakra turned theming from the thing I expected to babysit into the thing that made every new feature obviously easy."
  image="/shipped/steven-secreti.png"
/>

He expected to hand-build most of their multi-part components: the tree node
cards, the canvas toolbar, the inspector panels. Instead, slot recipes let him
define all of it, every slot, every variant, every state, in one file using
semantic tokens. Typegen checked the slot names for him at compile time.

"The payoff I didn't see coming was at the consumer level," he says. Once a
recipe worked, adding a new variant, or even a new component, took one or two
files instead of five.

## Moving from v2 to v3

Moving to v3 came with one snag.

<QuoteCard
  quote="The framework wasn't the problem. My habits were."
  image="/shipped/steven-secreti.png"
/>

The only thing that tripped him up was his own habits. After three years on v2,
he kept reaching for the old way of using chakra.

However, after spending some time to read the v3 docs, he was able to rebuild a
few components properly, using createSystem, semantic tokens, and recipes, until
the new patterns stuck. "A week in, v3 felt more natural than v2 ever had," he
says, "and I stopped missing the old way entirely."

## Advice for developers

His advice for developers: go for it, and commit to the token and recipe system
from day one. The biggest mistake people make is treating Chakra like a box of
ready-made components, then switching to inline styles the moment they need
something custom.

"You end up with the same scattered, unmaintainable styling you'd have had
without it," he says.

To get the best from Chakra UI, treat the theme like a contract. Define your
tokens, build your variants as recipes, and let everything else consume them.
They built an opinionated, dark-first design system and a dense canvas UI on top
of v3, and it never felt like a fight.
